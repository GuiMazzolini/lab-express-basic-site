About

Chico Mendes was born on December 15, 1944 in the Puerto Rico rubber plantation, near the border between Acre and Bolivia, in Xapuri, state of Acre. The son of a rubber tapper spent his childhood and youth beside his father cutting rubber.

Life on the rubber plantation shaped the young rubber tapper into a feeling of revolt against injustice. The economic activity of rubber extraction in the Amazon has always been guided by relationships of large exploitation. Aviamento, a system of exchanging industrial goods for extractive products, created a society in permanent misery and indebtedness. Rebellions were quelled by the violence of police forces. And a rigid regulation of subordination to the rubber tappers, the owners of the rubber plantations, punished those who dared to disrespect them with physical punishment.

Unlike other rubber tappers, however, at the age of 16 Chico learned to read, write and think with Euclides Fernandes Távora, a political refugee who lived close to his family's settlement. This fact had a great influence on his life. When unions began to be formed in Acre, he was aware that the time had come to change the reality of the rubber plantations. In 1975, he was part of the board of the Rural Workers Union of Brasiléia, the first created in Acre, presided over by the combative leader Wilson Pinheiro.

The situation in Acre was critical in the 1970s. The policy for the Amazon implemented by the military regime generated major land conflicts. The replacement of rubber by livestock led to land speculation and the deforestation of large tracts of land, preventing the rubber tappers from remaining in the forest.

In 1976, under the leadership of Wilson Pinheiro, the rubber tappers invented “defeating draws”: they gathered their families, went to areas threatened by deforestation, dismantled the peon camps and stopped the chainsaws. As a result of this resistance movement, in 1980 Wilson Pinheiro was murdered inside the union's headquarters in Brasiléia.
